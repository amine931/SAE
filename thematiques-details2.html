Themes of Expo 2026

Explore in detail the four main themes of our "Manuals of the World" exhibition

Colonization and decolonization

Multiple Perspectives on Colonial Expansion

This section of the exhibition examines how textbooks around the world present periods of colonization and decolonization, revealing radically different perspectives across countries and eras.

The gaze of the colonizers

Textbooks from former colonial powers (France, the United Kingdom, Spain, Portugal, etc.) have long presented colonization as a "civilizing mission" or a modernizing enterprise. The exhibition presents the evolution of these narratives, from imperialist justifications to the more critical approaches of contemporary textbooks.

The voice of the colonized

The Voice of the ColonizedIn contrast, textbooks from formerly colonized countries tell a different story, emphasizing resistance, exploitation, and the struggle for independence. Examples from Africa, Asia, and Latin America illustrate how these nations constructed their national identities through teaching about this period.

Independence movements

This section explores how liberation movements and decolonization figures are presented in school textbooks. Leaders such as Gandhi, Lumumba, Ho Chi Minh, and Nkrumah appear as national heroes or revolutionaries depending on the perspective.

Colonial heritage and memory

The section concludes with an analysis of how colonial heritage is taught today, revealing the persistent tensions between recognizing historical injustices and constructing a coherent national narrative.

The World Wars

Conflicts, multiple narratives

This section presents how the two world wars are taught through different national and cultural perspectives, revealing sometimes radically different interpretations of the same historical events.

The First World War

The exhibition compares accounts of the Great War in European textbooks, where it is often presented as an absurd and devastating tragedy, with those in the United States, where the emphasis is placed on America's entry into the war as a decisive moment. Textbooks from former colonies show how their soldiers were drawn into a conflict that was not their own.

The interwar period and the rise of totalitarianism

This section examines how textbooks from different countries explain the rise of fascism, Nazism, and Stalinism. There are striking contrasts between Western, Russian, and Asian approaches to this crucial period.

World War II

Narratives of World War II vary considerably across countries, with emphasis placed on different theaters of operations (Europe, Pacific), different turning points (Stalingrad, Pearl Harbor, D-Day), and different aspects (Holocaust, atomic bombs) depending on national perspectives.

Competing Memories

The section concludes with an analysis of the competing memories of these conflicts and how they continue to influence international relations today. Examples of recent textbook controversies in East Asia illustrate these persistent memory tensions.

History in the Digital Age

The digital revolution in history teaching

This section explores how digital technologies are transforming history teaching and our relationship with the past, offering new possibilities but also new challenges.

From paper manual to digital resources

The exhibition traces the evolution of teaching materials, from traditional textbooks to interactive digital platforms. Examples of digital textbooks from around the world illustrate the different approaches adopted by educational systems.

Virtual reality and immersive storytelling

This section presents innovative projects using virtual and augmented reality to bring history to life for students. Visitors can experience some of these applications, such as a reconstruction of ancient Rome or an immersion in the trenches of the First World War.

Digital archives and democratization of knowledge

The exhibition highlights how the digitization of historical archives allows unprecedented access to primary sources. Projects such as Europeana and the Digital National Archives of various countries demonstrate how these resources are integrated into teaching.

Challenges and prospects

The section also addresses the challenges posed by the digital revolution: the digital divide between countries and among students, historical misinformation on the internet, and issues of long-term preservation of digital resources. It concludes with a reflection on the future of history teaching in the age of artificial intelligence.

Propaganda or education?

When ideology shapes teaching

This section examines the sometimes blurred line between education and indoctrination, through the analysis of textbooks produced under different political regimes and at different times.

Textbooks under totalitarian regimes

The exhibition features striking examples of history textbooks produced under Nazism, fascism, Stalinism, and other authoritarian regimes of the 20th century. These documents reveal how history was used to legitimize the ruling power and shape the minds of younger generations.

Nationalism and identity construction

This section explores how, even in democracies, school textbooks have often served to construct a national identity, sometimes at the cost of simplifications or omissions. Examples from different countries show how these national narratives have evolved over time.

GTextbook Wars and Contemporary Controversies

The exhibition highlights recent controversies surrounding history textbooks, such as tensions between China, Japan, and Korea over the teaching of World War II, or debates in the United States over the teaching of slavery and segregation.

Towards a critical and plural history

The section concludes with a presentation of contemporary approaches aimed at developing students' critical thinking skills in the face of historical narratives. Examples of innovative textbooks encouraging multiperspectivity and source analysis illustrate this pedagogical evolution.
